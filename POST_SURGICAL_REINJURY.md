The likelihood of re-injury (re-rupture) following a distal biceps tendon repair is generally low, estimated between 1% and 5%. Most failures occur during the first few weeks due to non-compliance with post-operative restrictions or excessive force on the new repair.

Re-Injury Risk Breakdown by Week
Timeframe 	Re-Injury Likelihood & Risks	Activity & Protection
Weeks 1–3	Highest Risk (~1.5%+): Most reported re-ruptures occur within this initial window.	Arm is typically in a splint or sling. The focus is on protecting the surgical site from sudden, forceful contractions.
Weeks 4–8	Decreasing Risk (Moderate): Rare failures (around 3%) are still observed during this phase, often in chronic cases.	Splint is often removed. Patients may begin gentle, active range of motion but are strictly limited to light lifting (e.g., 1–5 lbs).
Weeks 9–12	Low Risk: The tendon is significantly more stable, but high-impact or heavy resistance can still cause failure.	Formal strengthening begins. Return to contact sports or high-impact work is usually restricted until at least 12 weeks.
Weeks 13–26	Very Low Risk: Re-injury is rare and typically requires a new traumatic event rather than normal activity.	Gradual return to full activity; heavy lifting and competitive sports are often cleared between 4 and 6 months.
Key Risk Factors
Non-compliance: The primary cause of early failure is failing to follow the rehabilitation protocol, such as lifting heavy objects too soon.
Smoking: Research indicates that smoking significantly increases the risk of re-rupture and other surgical complications.
Surgical Timing: "Chronic" repairs (performed >4 weeks after the initial injury) can have slightly higher complication rates due to tendon retraction.
Secondary Complications: While re-rupture is rare, other issues like nerve injury (affecting sensation or movement) are more common, occurring in up to 20% of cases.
